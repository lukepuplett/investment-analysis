## Executive Summary

**BLUF: BUY (with conviction) — Rolls-Royce is a compelling deep-value recovery story in mid-cycle of operational turnaround, with 3–5 year runway for margin expansion and cash generation. Supply-constrained aerospace demand through 2027 provides tailwind; LTSA recurring revenue base is defensive and expanding; UltraFan represents credible narrowbody opportunity (15–20% market share potential). Financial transformation is real (debt down £1.7B, FCF +286%, margin +1,500 bps since 2022); balance sheet fortress-built. Valuation is reasonable (17x P/E on depressed 2025 earnings, ex-growth; 4.7x P/S) for a company growing 8–10% with 22–24% operating margins by 2028. Key execution risks are UltraFan certification and supply chain, but not existential. 12–18 month catalyst: UltraFan first flight and OEM interest signals. 3–5 year upside: £2,000–2,500 (20–50% IRR) if base case executes. Downside protection: Aftermarket LTSA base and net cash position.**

## I. Investment Thesis Pillars

### Pillar 1: Operational Turnaround (Proven, Continuing)

**Core Thesis:**
Rolls-Royce has completed financial restructuring (2022–2025) and demonstrated sustainable operational improvement. Transition from distressed equity (£6B deficit) to profitability (£5.8B earnings) in 3 years validates management's strategy.

**Evidence:**
- ✅ Revenue +57% (£13.5B → £21.2B) with 16% 3-year CAGR
- ✅ Operating margin +1,500 bps (5.8% → 20.8%) — nearly all divisions contributing
- ✅ FCF generation 286% growth (£928M → £3.6B)
- ✅ ROIC ~19% (best-in-class for manufacturing)
- ✅ Debt reduction £1.7B (£5.96B → £4.3B)
- ✅ Net cash position achieved (£1.8B)

**Key Driver:** Operational efficiency + segment restructuring (power systems 12x margin improvement) + LTSA margin expansion (19 pp contract margin path).

**Confidence Level:** 9/10 (High). Turnaround is real, not accounting trick. Cash flows validate profit claims. Multiple divisions contributing (reduces single-point risk).

**Risk if Wrong:** Margin expansion slows if supply chain cost inflation outpaces pricing; execution stumbles on production ramp → growth stalls at 5–7% vs. 8–10% guided.

**Monitoring:** Quarterly operating profit vs. guidance; LTSA margin realization; FCF generation (must sustain £3.0B+).

---

### Pillar 2: Supply-Constrained Aerospace Demand (2025–2027 Tailwind)

**Core Thesis:**
Commercial aviation industry in best demand/supply environment in 30 years. Narrowbody engine shortage (demand ~2,400/year; supply ~1,800/year) supports pricing power, order visibility, and margins through 2027. Post-pandemic fleet modernization + fleet retirements creating tailwind.

**Evidence:**
- ✅ Airbus A320 production target 550+ aircraft/year (2,200+ engines)
- ✅ Boeing 737 production 500+ aircraft/year (1,000+ engines)
- ✅ All major OEMs running at max capacity; supply shortage
- ✅ Engine backlog 5–8 years (revenue visibility)
- ✅ Airline fuel hedges locking in robust capex plans

**Key Driver:** Supply shortage (CFM, P&W, RYCEY all constrained) → premium pricing, margin stability, strong order flow.

**Confidence Level:** 8/10 (High). Industry consensus clear. Supply shortage evident through 2026–2027.

**Risk if Wrong:** Recession hits in 2026–2027 → capex deferred, demand softens → 20–30% revenue decline from peak.

**Monitoring:** Airline capacity utilization %, orders backlog, OEM production guidance, credit spreads (recession indicator).

**Timeline Dependency:** This pillar expires 2027–2028 (supply normalizes). Longer-term growth requires UltraFan success.

---

### Pillar 3: LTSA Recurring Revenue Base (Defensive, Growing)

**Core Thesis:**
Long-term service agreements (LTSA) create 30–50 year revenue stream from engine purchase. Non-discretionary maintenance drives demand resilience through cycles. Margin expansion (19 pp contract margin path through 2028) flows through to P&L.

**Evidence:**
- ✅ LTSA revenue growing 20%+ annually (fastest-growing segment)
- ✅ Contract margins expanding as fleet ages (better utilization, predictable overhauls)
- ✅ Switching costs high (OEM-specific parts, certification, training)
- ✅ Aftermarket represents 40–50% of civil aerospace revenue (sticky base)
- ✅ Spare parts pricing power (supply-constrained)

**Key Driver:** Fleet age (A350, B787 now 5–10 years old) requiring increasing maintenance spend; LTSA contract terms locking in pricing for decades.

**Confidence Level:** 9/10 (Very High). LTSA is proven model. Aftermarket is structural revenue base.

**Risk if Wrong:** Spare parts cost inflation outpaces pricing power → margin dilution. Customer utilization drops (recession) → LTSA volume declines.

**Monitoring:** Quarterly LTSA margin (% of civil aero), spare parts volume, fleet utilization %, customer satisfaction (contract renewals).

**Sustainability:** LTSA grows with fleet size and age; structural over 30–50 year horizon. De-risks cyclical exposure.

---

### Pillar 4: UltraFan Narrowbody Opportunity (High-Risk, High-Reward)

**Core Thesis:**
UltraFan represents credible entry into $30B+ narrowbody market where RYCEY currently has only 15–20% share. Technology differentiation clear (fuel burn advantage), demonstrator underway, OEM qualification 2026–2027. Success = 15–20% market share, +£2–3B revenue by 2030.

**Evidence:**
- ✅ Narrowbody demand 2,400+ engines/year (largest market)
- ✅ UltraFan technical specifications superior (fuel burn -25% vs. CFM56)
- ✅ Demonstrator in build; first flight Q1 2026
- ✅ OEM interest signaled (management commentary)
- ✅ Government backing (UK support for advanced propulsion)
- ⚠️ Unproven architecture (open rotor not in service at scale since 1980s)
- ⚠️ Certification timeline aggressive (2027 target vs. 3–4 year typical)

**Key Driver:** Market share capture (UltraFan wins as proven alternative to LEAP/GTF) + scale (each 5% market share = £2–3B annual revenue at full ramp).

**Confidence Level:** 6/10 (Moderate). Technology credible but unproven. Execution risk real.

**Risk if Wrong:** Certification delayed 2–3 years → window closes, LEAP/GTF entrenched → RYCEY market share capped at 10–15%. Opportunity cost £2–3B in avoided revenue.

**Monitoring:** Flight test schedule, OEM qualification letters, competitor product launches (LEAP evolution, GTF new variants).

**Time Sensitivity:** Critical catalyst 2026 (first flight), 2027 (OEM selection), 2028–2030 (ramp production). Results in 3–5 year window.

---

### Pillar 5: Defense & Strategic Growth Optionality (SMRs, Battery Storage)

**Core Thesis:**
Defense revenue provides counter-cyclical stability (geopolitical tailwinds support budgets). SMR technology represents £50B+ future market opportunity if commercialization successful. Battery storage = smaller but growing adjacent market.

**Evidence:**
- ✅ Defense budgets rising (NATO, UK, Europe responding to Russia, China)
- ✅ Submarine programs (UK Astute, French Barracuda) multi-decade contracts
- ✅ Eurofighter sustainment through 2050+
- ✅ SMR market emerging (net-zero targets, industrial heat demand)
- ✅ Government backing (UK, EU, US supporting SMR development)
- ⚠️ Defense 8–10% of revenue (not core driver near-term)
- ⚠️ SMR pre-commercial (no revenue before 2030, high tech risk)

**Key Driver:** Geopolitical stability → defense budgets growing. SMR commercialization (2030+) = option value on £1–2B TAM by 2035.

**Confidence Level:** 7/10 (Moderate-High). Defense is proven; SMR is high-risk option.

**Risk if Wrong:** SMR technology fails or commercializes slower than 2030 → £200–300M sunk R&D. Defense budget cuts (peace breaks out?) → counterintuitive but possible.

**Monitoring:** Defense contract wins, SMR development milestones, government funding levels, geopolitical risk index.
